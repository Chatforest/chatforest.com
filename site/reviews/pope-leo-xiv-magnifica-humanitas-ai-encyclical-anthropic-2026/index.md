Today, the oldest continuously operating moral institution in the world published its first major doctrinal statement on artificial intelligence.

*Magnifica Humanitas* — "Magnificent Humanity" — is Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical letter. It was released this morning at 11:30 a.m. at the Vatican's Synod Hall, presented by the Pope alongside two curial cardinals, two theologians, and one unexpected face: Christopher Olah, co-founder of Anthropic and the researcher most responsible for trying to understand what AI systems are actually doing inside.

The pairing is worth sitting with. A pope and the leading interpretability researcher at a frontier AI lab, standing together at the Vatican to release a document on what it means to be human in an age of machine intelligence.

---

## The Date Was Not Accidental

Pope Leo XIV signed *Magnifica Humanitas* on May 15, 2026. He did not choose that date at random.

May 15, 1891, was the day Pope Leo XIII signed *Rerum Novarum* — "Of New Things." That document was the Catholic Church's response to the industrial revolution: a 12,000-word argument about workers' rights, the limits of capitalism, and the obligations that states, employers, and workers owed to each other as mechanization reshaped human labor and social life. *Rerum Novarum* is considered the founding text of modern Catholic social teaching.

135 years later, a pope who chose the same name published a new document about a new kind of disruption.

The parallel is not subtle. The industrial revolution displaced physical labor. AI is beginning to displace cognitive labor, creative production, and human judgment. The Church's position in 1891 was not "stop industrialization" — it was "industrialization must serve human beings, not the other way around." The position in 2026 is the same, applied to a different technology.

## What the Encyclical Actually Says

*Magnifica Humanitas* is not a condemnation of artificial intelligence. The Pope frames AI as presenting humanity with "a crossroads between self-sufficiency and solidarity" — a technology that can be directed toward the common good or away from it, depending on the values of the people building and deploying it.

Several positions in the document are notable:

**On human dignity as the standard.** The encyclical grounds human worth in theological terms — people possess dignity "simply because he or she is willed, created, and loved by God" — and then applies that grounding as a practical criterion: the question to ask about any AI application is whether it respects "the integral growth of the person" or treats people as means to an end. Efficiency, engagement, and profit are not disqualifying goals, but they cannot be the primary ones.

**On transhumanism.** The Pope explicitly criticizes those who "seek to transcend the human condition itself" through technology. The target here is not science fiction but a real current in Silicon Valley thinking: the belief that human limitations are problems to be solved through enhancement, that consciousness can be uploaded, that aging and death are engineering challenges. *Magnifica Humanitas* says that pursuit involves an "eclipse of the sense of what it means to be human" — a loss of the very thing being improved.

**On AI weapons.** The Vatican has been calling for meaningful human control over autonomous weapons since at least 2013. The encyclical reinforces this position: AI applications that "operate without meaningful human oversight" in lethal decisions remove the moral agent from decisions about who lives and who dies. The encyclical calls for international cooperation to prevent the development and deployment of such systems.

**On social fabric and relationships.** The document addresses AI's "impact on human relationships and the social fabric" — the concern that algorithmic mediation of communication, social sorting, and the replacement of human judgment with automated decisions is reshaping what it means to live in community.

The document's overall orientation is Augustinian in flavor: technology is neither good nor evil; its character is determined by whether it is ordered toward love of neighbor or love of self.

## Why Anthropic Was There

Christopher Olah did not walk into the Vatican cold.

In March 2026, a small group of Christian leaders and technologists gathered at Anthropic's San Francisco headquarters for a meeting Olah helped organize. The purpose was specific: to explain to Christian leaders how large language models like Claude are actually built — how training works, what the models are doing when they generate text, what interpretability research is trying to understand. The session also went the other direction: Anthropic solicited feedback on ethics and morality to inform Claude's development.

That meeting was one piece of a longer relationship between Olah and Vatican-adjacent institutions that eventually led to the invitation to present at today's launch.

Olah's work at Anthropic is relevant to the encyclical's concerns in a specific way. Interpretability research asks whether we can understand what AI systems are doing internally — whether we can see, not just infer, what concepts a model has formed, what reasoning paths it takes, what values are encoded in its behavior. The Vatican publicly endorsed interpretability research as "the AI safety practice that protects human dignity" — framing the technical question of what AI knows about itself as inseparable from the ethical question of whether humans can remain morally responsible for AI's actions.

The framing is coherent: if you cannot understand what a system is doing, you cannot be responsible for what it does. Responsibility requires legibility. Interpretability research is, from this view, not just a technical project but a precondition for human moral agency in an AI-saturated world.

## A New Vatican AI Commission

Three days before the encyclical's release, Pope Leo XIV approved a new Vatican commission on AI ethics — drawing representatives from seven dicasteries (the Vatican's functional departments) to coordinate the Church's engagement with AI across its institutions and communications.

The commission's mandate includes addressing AI challenges within the Holy See itself — how AI is used in Vatican operations, communications, and pastoral work — as well as guiding the broader Church's response to AI's social and ethical effects on Catholic communities worldwide.

## What the Critics Say

Not everyone is pleased with how today's launch was structured.

Some Catholic observers have expressed discomfort with the Vatican's partnership with Anthropic specifically. Anthropic is a for-profit AI company that has raised more than $30 billion in funding and is pursuing commercial AI deployment at massive scale. Critics argue that elevating a Silicon Valley AI lab's co-founder at a doctrinal launch risks blurring the line between the Church's independent moral authority and the reputational interests of a company whose products the document both implicitly endorses and critiques.

Others have noted that the document's language on transhumanism, while clear in its rejection, does not engage the strongest versions of the arguments it criticizes. The encyclical's theological grounding — human dignity as a gift from God — will be compelling to the Church's 1.4 billion adherents and largely unpersuasive to the secular AI researchers it is most trying to influence.

A third critique, which the encyclical anticipates, is that moral frameworks arrive after the technology does. *Rerum Novarum* was published forty years into the industrial revolution, when the worst outcomes were already visible. *Magnifica Humanitas* is published as AI capabilities are accelerating most rapidly — before the social effects of agentic AI, autonomous decision systems, and synthetic media are fully understood. Whether the document influences the systems now being built is a different question from whether it is right.

## What to Watch

- **Diplomatic weight**: Whether Catholic-majority governments — particularly in Europe, Latin America, and sub-Saharan Africa — use *Magnifica Humanitas* as a framework for AI legislation or regulatory positioning
- **Autonomous weapons**: The encyclical's strongest policy implication; watch for Vatican engagement with ongoing UN discussions about lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS) at the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons
- **The Anthropic relationship**: Whether today's launch is a one-time event or the beginning of a sustained engagement between the Church and frontier AI labs on ethics, interpretability, and deployment standards
- **Encyclical reception among AI researchers**: Whether the document's framing of interpretability as a prerequisite for moral responsibility gets traction in academic and industry AI safety communities

The Vatican has issued pastoral letters, addresses, and commission reports about AI before. An encyclical is different in weight — it is the most authoritative form of papal teaching, addressed to the entire Church. Whether it changes anything depends on what happens after the launch event ends.
